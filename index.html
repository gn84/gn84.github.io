Wasserstein Distance: a Geometric metric for AI?

Giuseppe Napoli, 25 June 2026.

Almost every metric we use in machine learning compares numbers. Accuracy is a count. A loss is an average. Even in algorithmic fairness, we usually look at scalar differences. Optimal transport asks a fundamentally deeper question: given two entire probability distributions, what is the mathematically cheapest way to morph one into the other?

This same lens is the right one in a surprising number of domains, from straightening the trajectories of state-of-the-art generative models to rigorously auditing algorithms for bias.

From Monge to Kantorovich

In 1781, Gaspard Monge asked a highly literal question: if you have a pile of sand shaped like A and you need to reshape it into B, what is the most efficient plan? You must move every grain, and moving a grain costs in proportion to the distance it travels. The minimum total cost of that reshaping is a distance between the two shapes, historically known as the Earth Mover's Distance.

Monge wanted a direct map sending each source point to a single target point. The math here is brutal: such a map might not exist (you can't split a single atom of mass with a pure function), and the optimization is strictly non-convex.

In 1942, Leonid Kantorovich relaxed the problem beautifully. Instead of a strict map, he proposed looking for a transport plan $\gamma$, a joint distribution that allows the mass of one point to be split across multiple destinations. The only constraints are the marginals.

$$W_p(\mu, \nu) = \left( \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu,\nu)} \int c(x,y)^p \, d\gamma(x,y) \right)^{1/p}$$

With a Euclidean cost and $p=2$, you get the 2-Wasserstein distance ($W_2$). This is a true metric on the space of probability distributions, symmetric and obeying the triangle inequality. Unlike a pseudo-divergence, its unit matches the unit of the domain itself, making the distance physically and mathematically interpretable.

Why KL Divergence Fails

Take two narrow, identical bumps, one on the left and one on the right, with no overlap. Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence and Total Variation see them as maximally different, regardless of how far apart the peaks actually are. They compare densities vertically, point by point, and instantly saturate where one density is zero and the other isn't.

Wasserstein, instead, measures how far you must slide the mass horizontally to line them up. It grows smoothly as the peaks drift apart.

This horizontal perspective is exactly why optimal transport resurrected deep generative models via the Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017). By swapping Jensen-Shannon for $W_1$, the generator receives a clean, usable gradient even when real and fake samples live on completely disjoint manifolds, the exact regime where classic GANs collapse. The dual form makes this computationally practical:

$$W_1(\mu, \nu) = \sup_{\|f\|_L \le 1} \left( \mathbb{E}_\mu[f] - \mathbb{E}_\nu[f] \right)$$

The Computational Bottleneck and the Sinkhorn Fix

The discrete Kantorovich problem is a linear program with $O(n^3 \log n)$ complexity. On a standard 50k deep learning batch, it is dead on arrival.

Marco Cuturi (2013) changed the game entirely with entropic regularization:

$$OT_\epsilon(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{\gamma} \left( \sum_{i,j} \gamma_{ij} C_{ij} - \epsilon H(\gamma) \right)$$

Adding an entropy term makes the objective strictly convex. The solution factorizes, reducing the entire optimization to Sinkhorn iterations: simple, alternating matrix-vector products that normalize rows and columns. This is massively parallelizable on GPUs, end-to-end differentiable, and fits into a few lines of JAX:

import jax, jax.numpy as jnp
from jax import jit, lax
from jax.scipy.special import logsumexp

@jit
def sinkhorn(C, a, b, eps=0.01, n_iter=200):
    # C: (n,m) cost matrix; a, b: marginals
    log_a, log_b = jnp.log(a), jnp.log(b)
    f, g = jnp.zeros_like(a), jnp.zeros_like(b)

    def step(carry, _):
        f, g = carry
        f = eps * (log_a - logsumexp((g[None, :] - C) / eps, axis=1))
        g = eps * (log_b - logsumexp((f[:, None] - C) / eps, axis=0))
        return (f, g), None

    (f, g), _ = lax.scan(step, (f, g), None, length=n_iter)
    P = jnp.exp((f[:, None] + g[None, :] - C) / eps)
    return jnp.sum(P * C), P

The 1D Exploit

For one-dimensional distributions (like scalar model predictions, age distributions, or any single continuous feature) optimal transport offers a massive computational shortcut. In 1D, the optimal map simply matches the quantiles. You sort both arrays and pair them up.

$$W_2(\mu, \nu)^2 = \int_0^1 (Q_\mu(t) - Q_\nu(t))^2 \, dt$$

There is no $\epsilon$, no entropic bias, and no hyperparameters. Just a sort. In JAX, it is exact, vectorized, and practically free:

@jit
def w2_1d(x, y, n_q=512):
    # exact 1D W2: pair the quantiles, no regularization
    q  = (jnp.arange(n_q) + 0.5) / n_q
    qx = jnp.quantile(x, q)
    qy = jnp.quantile(y, q)
    return jnp.sqrt(jnp.mean((qx - qy) ** 2))

When you need to scale back up to multivariate cases (feature spaces, barycenters), Cuturi's JAX-native OTT library handles the geometries and provides the debiased Sinkhorn divergence in three lines:

from ott.geometry import pointcloud
from ott.tools import sinkhorn_divergence

# S_eps(a,b) = OT_eps(a,b) - 0.5*OT_eps(a,a) - 0.5*OT_eps(b,b)
out = sinkhorn_divergence.sinkdiv(pointcloud.PointCloud, Xa, Xb, epsilon=0.01)
S_eps = out.divergence

Generative AI and Flow Matching

Once you have a geometric, differentiable distance between distributions, the applications multiply. The most powerful modern use case is Flow Matching (Lipman et al., 2022).

Instead of learning a score function like standard diffusion models, you learn a velocity field that transports noise into data. By coupling samples via minibatch OT during training, you physically straighten the trajectories of the flow. This allows the model to generate high-fidelity samples in radically fewer steps, pushing the bleeding edge toward one-step generation (Optimal Flow Matching, 2024).

Fairness: Diagnostics over Mass Destruction

Optimal transport has also revolutionized algorithmic fairness, mostly in a corrective form (e.g., "total repair" methods that move a disadvantaged group's distribution toward a Wasserstein barycenter to force demographic parity).

While mathematically elegant, blindly repairing data by moving mass is dangerous. Pushing samples toward a barycenter can force them off the true data manifold and into extrapolation regions the model was never trained on.

A more rigorous approach uses $W_2$ strictly as a diagnostic certificate. Instead of mutating the underlying data, the Wasserstein distance measures exactly how far a protected group's distribution sits from a fair reference distribution. Because $W_2$ retains the unit of the domain, it yields a single, comparable figure that can track bias across different models, datasets, and iterations, bypassing the fragility of scalar fairness metrics (like disparate impact ratios) that break down when base rates shift.

The Horizon

The framework has real open problems. The curse of dimensionality is severe: estimating $W_2$ from high-dimensional samples scales poorly ($n^{-1/d}$), requiring massive amounts of data. Entropic bias must be carefully managed using the Sinkhorn divergence, and unbalanced OT is still required to properly handle datasets with extremely small minority populations.

Still, the core point stands: while standard metrics offer weak, incommensurable proxies for structural differences, optimal transport provides a true, unit-aware geometric distance. Whether you are straightening generative flows or certifying algorithmic fairness, $W_2$ is the ultimate measuring stick.

JAX (and its environment) vs the two sacred monsters

Giuseppe Napoli, 27 June 2026.

What JAX and its satellite libraries (Flax, Optax, Equinox and friends) actually change for research, now that PyTorch and TensorFlow have absorbed half of its ideas.

PyTorch and TensorFlow are the sacred monsters of deep learning: enormous, battle tested, everywhere. JAX is younger and smaller. So the honest question is not "which framework wins", but what JAX's design changes for a researcher, and how much of that edge survives now that PyTorch has quietly adopted most of JAX's best moves.

The functional core

JAX is built on one idea: pure functions plus composable transforms. You write a plain function on arrays, then apply grad for its gradient, vmap to batch it, jit to compile it through XLA, pmap or shard_map to spread it across devices. The transforms compose. vmap(grad(f)) gives per sample gradients; jit(vmap(jacrev(f))) gives batched Jacobians fused into a single kernel. For research that lives on derivatives (Jacobians, Hessians, sensitivity analysis, physics informed losses) this is not a convenience, it is the working surface.

The part people miss: this is no longer exclusive. PyTorch shipped the same idea as torch.func (formerly functorch), upstreamed into core since PyTorch 2.0, with grad, vmap, jacrev, jacfwd, hessian, jvp and vjp. And torch.compile now captures graphs and fuses kernels in the spirit of XLA. So "JAX can do per sample gradients and PyTorch cannot" is simply outdated.

JAX

import jax, jax.numpy as jnp

def loss(p, x, y):
    pred = x @ p
    return jnp.mean((pred - y) ** 2)

g = jax.jit(jax.vmap(jax.grad(loss), in_axes=(None, 0, 0)))

PyTorch (torch.func)

import torch
from torch.func import grad, vmap

def loss(p, x, y):
    pred = x @ p
    return torch.mean((pred - y) ** 2)

g = vmap(grad(loss), in_dims=(None, 0, 0))

Same idea, two frameworks. JAX adds jit (whole program XLA) and an explicit PRNG once randomness enters.

What stays different

The gap is no longer the feature list, it is the design. Four things still set JAX apart for research.

1. Functional purity is the default, not an add on. In JAX a model is a function from (params, input) to output, with no hidden state. That makes the transforms total: whatever you write is differentiable, batchable and compilable, without "this op does not support vmap" surprises.

2. Randomness is explicit. JAX uses a stateless PRNG: you thread a key and split it. Same key, same numbers, every run, on any device. PyTorch's vmap, by its own docs, does not use a stateless PRNG and falls back to a flag system, so reproducibility of randomized vectorized code is weaker.

3. Transforms compose with control flow. With lax.cond, lax.scan and lax.while_loop, data dependent branches and loops still vectorize and differentiate under vmap and grad. functorch openly lists this as a gap it is still investigating.

4. The whole program compiles. jit hands the entire function to XLA as one graph, which is why JAX is often the fastest backend on TPU and GPU (the Keras 3 benchmarks tend to agree). PyTorch reaches similar territory with torch.compile, but its native mode is eager.

The satellite libraries

JAX itself is deliberately tiny: arrays, autodiff, vmap, jit, and not much else. Everything you would expect from a full framework lives in small, single purpose libraries that snap together, and knowing what each one is for is half the battle.

For building models you pick a philosophy. Flax (and its newer, more Pythonic NNX API) is the de facto high level library, with an explicit module system and parameter dictionaries you can actually see and manipulate. Equinox takes the purer route: a model is just a PyTree you can call, differentiate and jit like any other function, so nothing ever leaves plain JAX.

For training, Optax expresses optimizers as composable gradient transformations: Adam, learning rate schedules, weight decay and gradient clipping are chained like Lego instead of being buried inside an optimizer object. Orbax handles checkpointing (saving, restoring and sharding parameters, even asynchronously), and chex provides the assertions and test utilities that keep numerical code honest.

For everything past plain networks there is a small scientific stack: Diffrax for differentiable ODE and SDE solvers, Optimistix for root finding and least squares, Lineax for linear solves, and BlackJAX for MCMC and Bayesian sampling. jaxtyping adds shape and dtype annotations, so a signature tells you the tensor shapes a function expects. The pattern never changes: each library does one thing, stays a pure function, and composes with grad, vmap and jit instead of fighting them.

Where the giants still win

PyTorch owns research mindshare for good reasons: eager execution is the easiest thing in the world to debug, dynamic shapes are painless, and the ecosystem is enormous (HuggingFace, pretrained models, every new paper with code). TensorFlow has receded from research but stays strong in production and deployment (TF Serving, TFLite, TF.js), and from version 2.16 it ships Keras 3 as its high level API. Keras 3 is the pragmatic peace treaty: one model definition that runs on JAX, PyTorch or TensorFlow, so you can prototype eagerly in PyTorch and then train on TPU through JAX without a rewrite.

Bottom line

If the work is standard architectures and fast prototyping, the monsters are smoother. If the work is novel layers, custom training loops, heavy scientific computing and reproducible experiments at scale, JAX's functional purity and total, composable transforms are a real multiplier, not a fashion. And thanks to torch.func and Keras 3, choosing JAX no longer means leaving the rest of the world behind.

The 90% rule

Giuseppe Napoli, 28 June 2026.

Why operations research and heuristics solve most business problems, and why chasing ML/DL for everything is a practical mistake.

There is a pattern that repeats in data teams. A business problem arrives, a neural network gets proposed, six months pass, and the result is a model that beats a simpler baseline by 3% on a metric nobody in operations actually uses. Meanwhile the business kept running on spreadsheets and gut feel. The problem was solvable in two weeks with a solver and a decent heuristic.

This is not an argument against machine learning. It is an argument about frequency. Most business problems are structurally well-posed optimization problems. They have clear objectives, finite decision variables, and constraints that a domain expert can write down on a napkin. For that class of problem, deterministic and semi-deterministic methods are faster to deploy, easier to explain to management, and stable enough to run in production without a data science team babysitting them daily.

What the 90% looks like

The problems that fill most business agendas are resource allocation, scheduling, routing, pricing, and inventory replenishment. These are the canonical domains of operations research, solved well since the 1950s. Linear programming handles allocation under linear constraints exactly. Mixed integer programming covers combinatorial choices. Network flow models handle logistics at scale. For cases where exact solvers are too slow or the problem is non-convex, metaheuristics close most of the gap.

Simulated Annealing, Tabu Search, Genetic Algorithms, and Variable Neighborhood Search are not exotic tools. They are workhorses. A well-tuned SA on a VRP instance with 500 stops typically gets within 2% of optimal in minutes. No training data required.

Consider inventory replenishment. The classical (s, S) policy is analytically derived from demand variability and lead time. It is interpretable, tunable by a planner, and provably near-optimal under stationary demand. A deep reinforcement learning agent for the same problem needs millions of simulated episodes, careful reward shaping, and a stability regime most production environments cannot guarantee. In most real supply chains, the (s, S) policy wins on total cost and certainly on deployment effort.

Determinism as a feature, not a limitation

Deterministic methods have an underrated property: they are auditable. When a purchasing manager asks why the system ordered 400 units instead of 250, a solver with explicit constraints gives a traceable answer. A neural network gives a vector of activations. In regulated industries, auditability is not a nice-to-have.

Semi-determinism extends this idea. You add a stochastic layer only where uncertainty genuinely matters: stochastic programming for demand scenarios, robust optimization for supply disruptions, Monte Carlo for risk quantification. The core decision logic stays deterministic and readable; uncertainty is handled explicitly and separately.

import numpy as np, scipy.stats

def reorder_point(mu_lead, sigma_lead, mu_d, sigma_d, service_level=0.95):
    mu_dlt    = mu_d * mu_lead
    sigma_dlt = np.sqrt(mu_lead * sigma_d**2 + mu_d**2 * sigma_lead**2)
    z = scipy.stats.norm.ppf(service_level)
    return mu_dlt + z * sigma_dlt
    # planners understand every term. no black box.

Where ML and DL actually win

Machine learning wins in three genuine cases. First, when the feature space is high-dimensional and non-linear in ways domain knowledge cannot capture: demand forecasting with thousands of SKUs, complex seasonality, external signals. Second, when the input is unstructured: text, images, documents. Third, when the environment is genuinely sequential and adaptive. Even then, a warm-started policy from a good heuristic usually beats one trained from scratch.

The AI overload problem

The real cost of over-applying ML is not computational, it is organizational. A complex model requires data infrastructure, a training pipeline, a monitoring system, a retraining schedule, and someone who understands it well enough to debug it when it breaks in production. A well-parameterized solver requires none of that. It requires domain knowledge, which the business already has.

Data teams that default to ML for every problem end up maintaining a zoo of models, each fragile in its own way. Teams that apply OR and heuristics to the 90% and reserve ML for the 10% where it genuinely adds value end up with systems that run, that people trust, and that the business can own independently of the data team.

Bottom line

Operations research is not old-fashioned. It is precise. Heuristics and metaheuristics are not approximations to be ashamed of: they are engineering-grade tools that solve real problems at real scale. The best data scientist in a business context is not the one who reaches for a transformer first, but the one who knows when a good solver and a solid heuristic are enough, and when they are not. That judgment is rarer than it should be.

The JKO scheme: diffusion as geometry

Giuseppe Napoli, 29 June 2026.

How Jordan, Kinderlehrer and Otto showed in 1998 that thermodynamics, partial differential equations, and the geometry of optimal transport are the same thing.

Before 1998, the Fokker-Planck equation was understood as a PDE (partial differential equation) that governs diffusion: how a probability distribution spreads over time under the combined effect of a drift and noise. It worked. It was used everywhere in statistical physics and stochastic control. But nobody had a satisfying geometric explanation of why diffusion takes the specific path it takes among all possible paths in the space of distributions.

The answer came from Richard Jordan, David Kinderlehrer, and Felix Otto, in a paper submitted in 1996 and published in 1998 in the SIAM Journal on Mathematical Analysis. Their result is one of those rare theorems that, once you see it, makes everything click. Diffusion is not just a process you compute with a PDE. It is a gradient flow, a steepest descent path in the space of probability measures, where the geometry is given by the Wasserstein-2 distance.

The variational scheme

The JKO argument is constructive. Instead of writing the PDE directly, start from a discrete approximation with time step $\tau$. Suppose you are at distribution $\rho_k$ at time $k\tau$. The next distribution $\rho_{k+1}$ is defined as the solution to a minimization problem:

$$\rho_{k+1} = \operatorname{argmin}_{\rho}\; \left( \mathcal{F}(\rho) + \frac{1}{2\tau}\, W_2^2(\rho,\, \rho_k) \right)$$

Two forces are in tension here. The first term, $\mathcal{F}(\rho)$, is the free energy of the system, typically the sum of an internal energy and a negative entropy term. It pulls the distribution toward equilibrium: more spread, more disordered, lower free energy. The second term, $\frac{1}{2\tau}W_2^2(\rho,\rho_k)$, is a Wasserstein proximity penalty. It says: the distribution cannot jump arbitrarily far from where it was. Moving probability mass costs work, and $W_2$ measures exactly how much.

The right way to read the scheme: at each step, the system finds the distribution that most reduces its energy, subject to the constraint that it cannot move farther than the geometric budget $W_2$ allows. The time step $\tau$ controls how tightly the budget is enforced.

As $\tau \to 0$, the sequence of minimizers $\{\rho_k\}$ converges to a continuous curve in the space of probability measures. Jordan, Kinderlehrer and Otto proved that this limiting curve satisfies the Fokker-Planck equation exactly. A discrete optimization scheme, iterated infinitely fast, recovers a continuous PDE. The two formulations are equivalent.

What Wasserstein geometry adds

The key move in the JKO construction is using $W_2$ rather than some other distance. This is not arbitrary. The Wasserstein-2 distance is the unique metric on probability measures that encodes the cost of physically transporting mass from one location to another. It gives the space of distributions a Riemannian-like structure, sometimes called the Otto calculus, in which gradients and geodesics have precise meanings.

In this geometry, the Fokker-Planck equation is literally the gradient flow of the free energy $\mathcal{F}$, in exactly the same sense that ordinary gradient descent in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the gradient flow of a scalar function. The analogy is exact, not metaphorical. The diffusion equation and gradient descent are the same mathematical object, instantiated in different metric spaces.

Why this matters now

The JKO scheme went from being a mathematical curiosity to being computationally central once Cuturi (2013) showed that $W_2$ could be approximated efficiently via entropic regularization and the Sinkhorn algorithm. Suddenly the scheme was tractable at the scale of machine learning.

The consequences are visible in the modern ML literature. Score-based diffusion models (DDPM, score matching, flow matching) are, at their core, learning the reverse of a gradient flow in distribution space. The Fokker-Planck PDE shows up in the analysis of Langevin MCMC sampling. Wasserstein gradient flows are used to train generative models by minimizing $W_2$ between the generated and the target distribution. The 1998 paper is the theoretical foundation for all of it.

More broadly, the JKO scheme offers a unifying language. Phenomena that look different at first (heat diffusion, Brownian motion, Langevin dynamics, score-based sampling) all fit the same template: a gradient flow of some energy functional in Wasserstein space. The PDE changes, the energy functional changes, but the geometric structure stays the same. That structural unity is what makes the framework generative rather than merely descriptive.

A JAX sketch

The snippet below is a mirror-descent approximation, not a true JKO step. In the real scheme $\rho_{k+1}$ is the unknown of a joint variational problem: energy and Wasserstein proximity are minimized together with no pre-determined target. This approximation tracks the Fokker-Planck flow when $\tau \to 0$ and $\mathcal{F}$ is nearly flat; a faithful discretization requires an inner loop (e.g. JKO-ICNN, Mokrov et al. 2021):

!pip install ott-jax -q

import jax.numpy as jnp
from ott.geometry import pointcloud
from ott.problems.linear import linear_problem
from ott.solvers.linear import sinkhorn

def mirror_descent_step(rho_k, log_grad_F, tau, eps=0.01):
    """
    Single mirror-descent step approximating a JKO step
    in the space of probability measures via entropic OT.
    Args:
        rho_k      : current distribution (1D array, shape [N,])
        log_grad_F : log of the first variation of F (1D array)
        tau        : step size
        eps        : entropic regularization parameter
    Returns:
        rho_next   : updated probability distribution
    """
    N    = rho_k.shape[0]
    x    = jnp.linspace(0, 1, N)[:, None]
    geom = pointcloud.PointCloud(x, x, epsilon=eps)

    # exponentiated gradient: proposed target (not the JKO minimizer)
    rho_proposed = jnp.exp(-tau * log_grad_F) * rho_k
    rho_proposed = rho_proposed / rho_proposed.sum()

    prob = linear_problem.LinearProblem(geom, a=rho_k, b=rho_proposed)
    out  = sinkhorn.Sinkhorn()(prob)

    # axis=0 -> target marginal (rho_next); axis=1 would return source (rho_k)
    return out.matrix.sum(axis=0)

# ── validation ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
N          = 100
rho_k      = jnp.ones(N) / N             # uniform start
log_grad_F = -jnp.linspace(0, 5, N)      # synthetic force pushing mass rightward
tau        = 0.5

rho_next     = mirror_descent_step(rho_k, log_grad_F, tau)
x_grid       = jnp.linspace(0, 1, N)
initial_mean = jnp.sum(rho_k    * x_grid)
new_mean     = jnp.sum(rho_next * x_grid)

print(f"Sum of new distribution (must be 1.0): {jnp.round(rho_next.sum(), 4)}")
print(f"Initial spatial mean : {jnp.round(initial_mean, 4)}")
print(f"Shifted spatial mean : {jnp.round(new_mean, 4)}")

Toward a universal theory

Step back from the details and a larger picture appears. Heat diffusing in a metal, ink spreading in water, a stochastic process relaxing to equilibrium: these are not unrelated events. They are the same kind of motion, a descent of some energy in a curved space of distributions, paying a geometric transport cost at every step. The extension to neural networks is more delicate. Standard SGD on weights is a gradient flow in parameter space $\mathbb{R}^d$ with Euclidean metric, not Wasserstein. The connection exists but is conditional: in the mean-field limit (wide one-layer networks) the distribution of neuron parameters becomes a Wasserstein gradient flow, a result made precise by Chizat and Bach (2018) and Mei, Montanari and Nguyen (2018). Outside that regime the analogy is a hint, not an identity. Thermodynamics, the physics of diffusion, and the dynamics of learning systems all seem to borrow from one shared rulebook.

This is why the optimal transport viewpoint keeps resurfacing across fields that were never supposed to meet. Free energy, entropy, gradient flows, and Wasserstein geometry are not metaphors imported into machine learning for flavor. They are the actual structure underneath, the same structure physicists found in statistical mechanics a century ago. When the same equations describe a gas, a diffusion, and a generative model, the equations are telling you something about all three at once.

It is hard not to read this as a hint. If the behavior of neural networks really is governed by the same variational and geometric principles that govern physical systems, then a universal theory of neural networks, one that explains why they train, generalize and converge in terms of energy, geometry and entropy rather than ad hoc engineering, is not a fantasy. It is a matter of time. The JKO scheme is one of the early bricks of that bridge, and the traffic across it is only getting heavier.
